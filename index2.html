Severe Features — Any One Qualifies

Criteria for Severe Features

  • BP ≥160/110 on 2 readings ≥4 hrs apart
  • Thrombocytopenia: platelets <100,000/μL
  • Renal insufficiency: Cr >1.1 mg/dL or doubling
  • Liver: AST/ALT ≥2× ULN ± RUQ/epigastric pain
  • Pulmonary edema
  • New-onset headache unresponsive to medication
  • Visual disturbances
HELLP Syndrome

Tennessee Classification

  • Hemolysis: LDH >600 U/L, abnormal smear, bilirubin >1.2
  • Elevated Liver enzymes: AST >70 U/L
  • Low Platelets: <100,000/μL
  • Partial HELLP if 1–2 criteria met
  • Deliver at ≥34 weeks regardless of severity
Eclampsia Response

Seizure Management

  • Protect airway · Left lateral decubitus
  • Mg₂SO₄ 4–6 g IV over 15–20 min (if not already on)
  • Rebolus 2 g if seizing on Mg
  • Lorazepam/diazepam if Mg fails
  • Stabilize → delivery plan (not emergent cesarean unless obstetric indication)
  • Continue Mg 24 hrs postpartum
Postpartum Surveillance

Post-Delivery Monitoring

  • BP check at 72 hrs and 7–10 days postpartum
  • Continue Mg 24 hrs after delivery
  • HTN can worsen postpartum (days 3–7)
  • Treat if BP ≥150/100 postpartum
  • NSAID caution if hypertensive
  • Counsel on lifelong CV risk

Case 1: The "Routine" Elevated BP
G1P0 · 36+2 weeks · Prenatal visit
Moderate Complexity

A 28-year-old G1P0 presents to her routine prenatal visit at 36+2 weeks. She has no complaints. Her prenatal course has been uncomplicated. On intake, BP is 148/96 mmHg. The nurse repeats it 20 minutes later: 152/98 mmHg.

What is your diagnosis, and what is the next step in management?
Diagnosis: Preeclampsia without severe features — BP ≥140/90 on two readings ≥4 hrs apart with proteinuria at ≥20 weeks.
Next steps: Admit for observation. Send CBC, CMP, LFTs, uric acid, 24-hour urine protein (or repeat urine Pr:Cr). Continuous fetal monitoring.
Delivery timing: At 36+2 weeks with preeclampsia without severe features, ACOG recommends delivery at ≥37+0 weeks. If labs are reassuring and BP remains below severe range, expectant management for ~5 more days is appropriate.
Key teaching point: No symptoms does not exclude preeclampsia. Proteinuria on dipstick requires quantification. Do NOT send home without evaluation — this presentation requires hospital-level workup.

What syndrome does this patient have, and what is your immediate management plan?
Diagnosis: HELLP syndrome (hemolysis: LDH 780, thrombocytopenia <100k, elevated LFTs ≥2× ULN) superimposed on chronic HTN, with severe-feature preeclampsia.
Immediate: IV access × 2. Acute severe HTN treatment NOW — labetalol 20 mg IV (she's already on PO labetalol; IV dosing is independent). Goal BP <160/110 within 30–60 min.
Magnesium: Load Mg₂SO₄ 4–6 g IV over 15–20 min → 2 g/hr maintenance for seizure prophylaxis. Monitor UO, DTRs, respiratory rate.
Steroids: Betamethasone 12 mg IM × 2 doses 24 hrs apart (31+5 wk — imminent delivery likely; fetal lung maturity benefit).
Delivery: HELLP at 31+5 weeks — stabilize, then deliver. At <34 weeks with HELLP, ACOG recommends delivery after corticosteroid course if maternal and fetal status stable. Call MFM/attending immediately.
Teaching: Platelets <100k with HELLP — do NOT give neuraxial anesthesia without hematology/anesthesia discussion. Platelet transfusion if <50k before surgery.

How do you manage this patient over the phone? What should she do, and what will you do?
Disposition: Send to L&D or ED immediately. Do not manage new postpartum hypertension by phone alone. BP 158/104 + headache = new-onset postpartum preeclampsia until proven otherwise.
On arrival: Repeat BP, CBC, CMP, LFTs, urine Pr:Cr. If BP ≥160/110, treat immediately. Consider Mg if severe features present (headache qualifies).
Teaching: Postpartum preeclampsia most commonly occurs days 3–6 after delivery. Up to 25% of eclampsia cases occur postpartum. New hypertension beyond 72 hrs postpartum warrants full workup regardless of prenatal history.
NSAIDs: Ibuprofen commonly prescribed postpartum — hold if BP is elevated, as NSAIDs can worsen hypertension and mask symptoms.

ACOG Practice Bulletin No. 222

Evidence-Based Guideline Deep-Dive

Grade A Good/consistent scientific evidence   Grade B Limited/inconsistent evidence   Grade C Expert opinion / consensus

Diagnosis

A
Preeclampsia is diagnosed by new-onset hypertension (≥140/90 mmHg) after 20 weeks, with proteinuria ≥300 mg/24h or Pr:Cr ≥0.3, OR any severe feature in the absence of proteinuria.
A
Proteinuria is not required for diagnosis if severe features are present (thrombocytopenia, renal insufficiency, hepatic involvement, pulmonary edema, new headache, or visual disturbances).
B
A 24-hour urine protein collection or a spot urine protein-to-creatinine ratio ≥0.3 are the preferred methods for quantifying proteinuria; dipstick alone is not sufficient for definitive diagnosis.
B
Uric acid, LDH, and haptoglobin are not diagnostic criteria, but rising uric acid may predict worsening disease. These labs inform clinical picture.

Management — Without Severe Features

A
Delivery at 37+0 weeks is recommended for preeclampsia without severe features. Expectant management before 37 weeks requires close maternal and fetal surveillance.
B
Antihypertensive therapy is not recommended for preeclampsia without severe features (BP 140–159/90–109) for maternal benefit, but may be used to allow prolongation of pregnancy.
B
Recommended monitoring includes weekly BMP, CBC, LFTs, and fetal surveillance with NST/BPP. Serial growth ultrasounds are indicated.

Management — With Severe Features

A
Delivery at ≥34+0 weeks is recommended. Before 34 weeks, hospitalization with close monitoring is required; expectant management is only appropriate in select, stable patients at tertiary centers.
A
Magnesium sulfate is recommended for seizure prophylaxis in women with preeclampsia with severe features and should be continued for at least 24 hours postpartum.
A
Acute severe hypertension (≥160/110) should be treated with antihypertensive medications within 30–60 minutes. Labetalol IV, hydralazine IV, and nifedipine IR PO are all first-line options.
B
Antenatal corticosteroids (betamethasone) are recommended for all women with preeclampsia with severe features at 24+0 to 36+6 weeks who are expected to deliver within 7 days.

Prevention

A
Low-dose aspirin 81 mg/day is recommended beginning 12–28 weeks (optimally before 16 weeks) through delivery for women with ≥1 high-risk factor or ≥2 moderate-risk factors.
B
Calcium supplementation ≥1 g/day may reduce risk in populations with low dietary calcium intake. Not routinely recommended in the US where calcium intake is generally adequate.
C
Vitamin C, vitamin E, and fish oil supplementation are not recommended for preeclampsia prevention. No evidence of benefit from randomized trials.

Long-Term Implications

A
Women with a history of preeclampsia have ~2× higher lifetime risk of ischemic heart disease, stroke, and thromboembolic disease. Preeclampsia is an independent cardiovascular risk factor.
B
Postpartum BP evaluation at 72 hours and 7–10 days after delivery is recommended. New antihypertensives may be needed; methyldopa should generally be avoided postpartum due to side effects.

First-Line
Loading Dose

Maintenance

Mg/nifedipine interaction: Concurrent use may cause excessive hypotension or neuromuscular blockade. Monitor closely.

Hydralazine
Arteriolar Vasodilator · IV Antihypertensive
Second-Line
Acute IV

10–30 minutes · Duration: 2–4 hours
Side Effects
Reflex tachycardia · Headache (can mimic eclampsia symptoms) · Unpredictable response · Palpitations
Unpredictable hypotensive response. Less preferred than labetalol or nifedipine as first-line per current ACOG guidance. Hydralazine-induced headache can complicate symptom assessment.
Betamethasone
Corticosteroid · Fetal Lung Maturity
Standard of Care
Dosing
12 mg IM × 2 doses, 24 hours apart
Indication Window
24+0 to 36+6 weeks if delivery anticipated within 7 days
Efficacy
Reduces RDS, IVH, necrotizing enterocolitis. Late preterm course (34–36+6) reduces NICU admission and respiratory morbidity.
Effect on BP
Can transiently worsen hypertension in first 24–48 hrs. Anticipate potential BP spike and monitor closely after administration.
Betamethasone can worsen hyperglycemia in diabetic patients. Monitor BG closely. Consider insulin adjustments. Effect lasts ~48–72 hours after completion.

🔴 High-Risk Factors — 1 or more → prescribe aspirin
  • History of preeclampsia (especially early-onset or severe)
  • Multifetal gestation (twins, triplets)
  • Chronic hypertension
  • Type 1 or Type 2 diabetes mellitus
  • Chronic kidney disease
  • Autoimmune disease (SLE, antiphospholipid syndrome)
🟡 Moderate-Risk Factors — 2 or more → prescribe aspirin
  • Nulliparity (first pregnancy)
  • Obesity (BMI >30 kg/m²)
  • Family history (mother or sister with preeclampsia)
  • Age 35 years or older
  • African American race/ethnicity
  • Low socioeconomic status
  • Prior adverse pregnancy outcome (FGR, placental abruption)
