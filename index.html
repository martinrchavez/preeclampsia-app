What is Preeclampsia?

Preeclampsia is a pregnancy complication characterized by high blood pressure and signs of damage to organ systems, most often the liver and kidneys. It typically occurs after 20 weeks of pregnancy and can affect both mother and baby.

Key Facts

üìä Prevalence

Affects 2-8% of pregnancies globally. In the United States, rates increased by 25% between 1987 and 2004.

‚ö†Ô∏è Severity

Responsible for approximately 16% of maternal deaths in high-income countries and up to 26% in Latin America and the Caribbean.

üí∞ Economic Impact

Estimated to cost $2.18 billion within the first 12 months of delivery in the United States (2012 data).

üë∂ Fetal Impact

Can lead to preterm delivery, fetal growth restriction, and other complications affecting the baby.

Understanding the Condition

Preeclampsia develops when the placenta doesn't function properly, leading to problems with blood flow. This can cause:

  • High blood pressure (hypertension) - Blood pressure of 140/90 mmHg or higher
  • Protein in urine (proteinuria) - Though not always present
  • Organ dysfunction - Affecting kidneys, liver, brain, or other organs
  • Blood abnormalities - Low platelet count or liver enzyme changes

üìö Important to Know

Most cases of preeclampsia occur in healthy first-time mothers with no obvious risk factors. However, having risk factors increases your chances of developing the condition, which is why early prenatal care and monitoring are crucial.

Signs and Symptoms of Preeclampsia

‚ö†Ô∏è Severe Features - Seek Immediate Care:

If you experience any of these symptoms, contact your healthcare provider immediately or go to the emergency room:

ü§ï

Severe Headache

Persistent headache that doesn't respond to acetaminophen (Tylenol) and is not explained by other causes. May feel like the worst headache of your life.

Blurred vision, seeing spots or flashing lights, temporary loss of vision, light sensitivity, or seeing "floaters."

Persistent pain in the right upper abdomen or below the ribs on the right side (where the liver is located). May also feel like severe heartburn or indigestion.

üìà

Systolic BP ‚â• 160 mmHg or diastolic BP ‚â• 110 mmHg. If you're monitoring at home and see these readings, contact your provider immediately.

ü´ß

Blood pressure of 140/90 mmHg or higher on two occasions at least 4 hours apart (if not on treatment already).

üíß

Your healthcare provider will check for this during prenatal visits. You won't notice this yourself.

ü¶µ

Rapid swelling of hands, face, or feet, especially if it occurs suddenly. Some swelling is normal in pregnancy, but sudden or severe swelling should be evaluated.

ü§Æ

Especially in the second half of pregnancy (after 20 weeks), or if accompanied by other symptoms. This can be a sign of HELLP syndrome.

üìû When to Contact Your Healthcare Provider

  • Any of the severe symptoms listed above
  • Blood pressure readings consistently above 140/90
  • Rapid weight gain (more than 2 pounds in a week)
  • Decreased fetal movement
  • Any concerns about your symptoms

üí° Important Notes

Preeclampsia can develop without warning signs. Some women with preeclampsia don't have symptoms until the condition is advanced. This is why regular prenatal care and blood pressure monitoring are essential.

Not all symptoms mean preeclampsia. Many symptoms of preeclampsia are common in normal pregnancy. Only your healthcare provider can diagnose preeclampsia through blood pressure checks, urine tests, and blood tests.

How is Preeclampsia Diagnosed?

Diagnostic Criteria

Preeclampsia is diagnosed when a pregnant woman after 20 weeks of gestation has:

Blood Pressure Criteria:

  • Systolic BP ‚â• 140 mmHg OR Diastolic BP ‚â• 90 mmHg on two occasions at least 4 hours apart
  • OR Systolic BP ‚â• 160 mmHg OR Diastolic BP ‚â• 110 mmHg (can be confirmed within minutes to allow quick treatment)

AND Either:

  • Proteinuria: ‚â•300 mg of protein in a 24-hour urine collection, OR protein/creatinine ratio ‚â•0.3, OR dipstick reading of 2+ (if other methods not available)

OR in the absence of proteinuria, new onset of any of the following:

  • Low platelet count: Less than 100,000 √ó 10‚Åπ/L
  • Kidney problems: Serum creatinine >1.1 mg/dL or doubling of creatinine
  • Liver problems: Liver enzymes elevated to twice normal levels
  • Pulmonary edema: Fluid in the lungs
  • Brain symptoms: New-onset headache not responsive to medication, OR visual disturbances

Types of Preeclampsia

Preeclampsia Without Severe Features

  • Blood pressure 140-159/90-109 mmHg
  • May have proteinuria
  • No severe symptoms
  • Normal lab results

Preeclampsia With Severe Features

  • Blood pressure ‚â•160/110 mmHg
  • Severe symptoms (headache, vision changes, abdominal pain)
  • Abnormal lab results (low platelets, high liver enzymes, kidney problems)
  • Pulmonary edema

HELLP Syndrome

HELLP syndrome is a severe form of preeclampsia characterized by:

  • Hemolysis (breakdown of red blood cells)
  • Elevated Liver enzymes
  • Low Platelet count

HELLP syndrome can develop suddenly and requires immediate medical attention. Common symptoms include right upper abdominal pain, nausea, vomiting, and general malaise.

Tests Your Provider May Order

ü©∏

Blood Tests

Complete blood count (CBC) to check platelets, liver function tests (AST, ALT), kidney function (creatinine), and lactate dehydrogenase (LDH)

üíß

Urine Tests

24-hour urine collection or protein/creatinine ratio to check for proteinuria

üë∂

Fetal Monitoring

Non-stress test (NST), biophysical profile, ultrasound to check baby's growth and amniotic fluid levels

ü´Ä

Blood Pressure Monitoring

Regular blood pressure checks at home or in clinic, sometimes continuous monitoring in hospital

‚ö†Ô∏è Important Information

Preeclampsia can occur without proteinuria. About 20% of women with preeclampsia don't have protein in their urine. Your provider diagnoses based on the complete clinical picture.

Symptoms don't always predict severity. Some women with severe preeclampsia have minimal symptoms, while others with mild preeclampsia may have concerning symptoms. This is why regular monitoring is crucial.

Managing Preeclampsia

üéØ Treatment Goal

The only cure for preeclampsia is delivery. However, the timing of delivery depends on the severity of the condition, gestational age, and maternal and fetal wellbeing. Management aims to:

  • Keep mother safe
  • Prevent complications (seizures, stroke, organ damage)
  • Allow the baby to develop as much as safely possible
  • Deliver at the right time for both mother and baby

Management Based on Severity and Timing

Preeclampsia Without Severe Features

At ‚â•37 weeks: Delivery is recommended

Before 37 weeks: Close monitoring with:

  • Weekly or twice-weekly blood pressure checks
  • Weekly blood tests (CBC, liver enzymes, creatinine)
  • Weekly or twice-weekly fetal monitoring (NST)
  • Ultrasound every 3-4 weeks for fetal growth
  • May be managed at home if stable

Preeclampsia With Severe Features

At ‚â•34 weeks: Delivery recommended after stabilization

Before 34 weeks: Hospitalization with:

  • Continuous or frequent blood pressure monitoring
  • Antihypertensive medications if BP ‚â•160/110
  • Magnesium sulfate to prevent seizures
  • Corticosteroids for fetal lung maturity
  • Close fetal monitoring
  • Delivery when condition worsens or at 34 weeks

Medications

üíä

Antihypertensive Medications

For severe hypertension (BP ‚â•160/110):

  • Labetalol (IV or oral) - Beta blocker that lowers blood pressure
  • Hydralazine (IV) - Vasodilator that relaxes blood vessels
  • Nifedipine (oral) - Calcium channel blocker

Treatment should begin within 30-60 minutes of confirmed severe hypertension.

üß™

Magnesium Sulfate

For seizure prevention in severe preeclampsia/eclampsia:

  • Administered IV during labor, delivery, and 24 hours postpartum
  • Reduces risk of eclamptic seizures by more than 50%
  • Most effective treatment proven by research
  • Requires monitoring of reflexes, breathing, and urine output
üíâ

Corticosteroids

For fetal lung maturity (if delivery expected before 34 weeks):

  • Betamethasone or dexamethasone
  • Helps baby's lungs mature faster
  • Reduces risk of respiratory distress syndrome and other complications
  • Most effective when given 24 hours before delivery

Delivery Considerations

Indications for Immediate Delivery (regardless of gestational age):

  • Eclampsia (seizures)
  • Uncontrolled severe hypertension despite medication
  • HELLP syndrome
  • Pulmonary edema (fluid in lungs)
  • Placental abruption
  • Non-reassuring fetal status
  • Fetal demise
  • Persistent severe symptoms

Mode of Delivery:

The mode of delivery (vaginal vs. cesarean) is based on standard obstetric indications, not preeclampsia itself. Many women with preeclampsia can have a vaginal delivery if conditions are favorable.

Postpartum Management

Preeclampsia doesn't always resolve immediately after delivery:

  • Blood pressure monitoring continues for 72 hours postpartum (or longer if hypertension persists)
  • Magnesium sulfate typically continued for 24 hours after delivery in severe cases
  • Some women develop preeclampsia for the first time postpartum
  • Blood pressure medications may be needed for weeks to months
  • Close follow-up is essential, especially in the first week postpartum

üö® Postpartum Warning Signs

Contact your healthcare provider immediately if you experience after delivery:

  • Severe headache
  • Vision changes
  • Severe abdominal pain
  • Difficulty breathing
  • High blood pressure (if monitoring at home)
  • Confusion or altered mental status

Eclampsia and stroke can occur in the postpartum period, especially within the first 48-72 hours.

Preventing Preeclampsia

Can Preeclampsia Be Prevented?

While there's no guaranteed way to prevent preeclampsia, research has identified interventions that can reduce risk in high-risk women. Early prenatal care and monitoring are essential for all pregnant women.

Low-Dose Aspirin

‚úÖ Recommended Prevention Strategy

Who should take aspirin: Women with high-risk factors (one or more) OR multiple moderate-risk factors

Dose: 81 mg daily (baby aspirin)

Timing: Start between 12-28 weeks of pregnancy (ideally before 16 weeks), continue until delivery

Effectiveness: Reduces risk of preeclampsia by 10-15%, with greater reduction in severe preeclampsia (40-50%) and preterm preeclampsia when started early

High-Risk Factors (Take aspirin if you have ONE or more):

  • History of preeclampsia
  • Multifetal gestation (twins, triplets)
  • Chronic hypertension
  • Type 1 or Type 2 diabetes
  • Kidney disease
  • Autoimmune disease

Moderate-Risk Factors (Take aspirin if you have TWO or more):

  • First pregnancy
  • Obesity (BMI >30)
  • Family history of preeclampsia
  • Age ‚â•35 years
  • African American race
  • Low socioeconomic status
  • Previous adverse pregnancy outcome

Calcium Supplementation

ü•õ

For Women with Low Calcium Intake

If your daily calcium intake is less than 600 mg/day, calcium supplementation (1.5-2 grams per day) may reduce preeclampsia risk. This is particularly relevant in developing countries but less applicable in the United States where calcium intake is typically adequate.

What Doesn't Work

‚ùå Not Recommended (Insufficient Evidence)

The following have been studied but are NOT proven effective for preventing preeclampsia:

  • Vitamins C and E - No benefit shown in clinical trials
  • Fish oil supplements - No reduction in preeclampsia risk
  • Vitamin D - Insufficient evidence
  • Folic acid (beyond standard prenatal dose) - No additional benefit
  • Garlic supplements - No proven benefit
  • Bed rest - Not effective and may increase other risks
  • Sodium restriction - Not recommended
  • Metformin, sildenafil, statins - Still under investigation, not recommended outside research trials

Lifestyle Modifications

Before Pregnancy

  • Achieve healthy weight if possible
  • Control chronic conditions (hypertension, diabetes)
  • Optimize nutrition
  • Discuss risks with your healthcare provider if you've had preeclampsia before

During Pregnancy

  • Attend all prenatal appointments
  • Take prenatal vitamins as prescribed
  • Monitor blood pressure as recommended
  • Report new symptoms immediately
  • Maintain healthy lifestyle (balanced diet, appropriate exercise)

Long-Term Cardiovascular Health

‚ö†Ô∏è Important for Future Health

Women who have had preeclampsia have approximately 2x higher risk of cardiovascular disease later in life, including:

  • Chronic hypertension
  • Heart disease
  • Stroke
  • Kidney disease

This risk is even higher if you had:

  • Preeclampsia requiring preterm delivery
  • Recurrent preeclampsia in multiple pregnancies
  • Early-onset preeclampsia (before 34 weeks)

üíö Reducing Long-Term Risk

After having preeclampsia, focus on cardiovascular health:

  • Regular check-ups: Monitor blood pressure and cardiovascular risk factors
  • Healthy weight: Achieve and maintain a healthy BMI
  • Exercise: Regular physical activity (aim for 150 minutes per week)
  • Healthy diet: Focus on fruits, vegetables, whole grains, lean protein
  • Don't smoke: Quit smoking if you smoke
  • Manage conditions: Control blood pressure, cholesterol, blood sugar
  • Inform providers: Always tell healthcare providers about your history of preeclampsia
